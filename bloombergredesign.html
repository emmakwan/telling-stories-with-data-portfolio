Redesigning a Bloomberg Payroll Data Visualization

The Original Visualization

The visualization I chose is from a Bloomberg article titled "'Crazy Mess' of Jobs Data Has Economists Getting It Wrong Again."

Critiquing the Visualization

I initially evaluated my dataset with the Data Visualization Effectiveness Profile developed by Stephen Few, which focused on a few categories.

Usefuless: 8/10

I gave the visualization an 8/10 because I thought it was a very useful visualization for the intended audience. The subject area is the economy and jobs, and especially with COVID and Omicron, I think that it's important that people are aware of the factors affecting the U.S. economy. I also thought it was useful because it was pushing back against a narrative that jobs are really down with COVID and could hopefully educate people on the current state of working.

Completeness: 6/10

I gave the visualization a 6/10 because while I think it was fairly complete, it still was missing some key context for what payroll data actually is and why we should find that information important. Although there wasn't any missing data necessarily, I found the missing context important enough to dock points off for completeness because the data doesn't tell a full story without context.

Perceptibility: 6/10

While I thought that the visualization was okay in terms of perceptibility, I thought that there was unnecessary effort that would need to be put in to truly understand what was going on. I also thought that the visualizatio format wasn't making the comparison as easy and that greatly affected perceptibility.

Truthfulness: 9/10

I did not think that the visualization was being untruthful at all. I could tell that the story the visualization was trying to tell was supported by the data that was being visualized.

Intuitiveness: 6/10

I think that this visualization was not as intuitive for people who are unfamiliar with labor statistics. For example, I at first thought that the y-axis was in dollars since I assumed payroll ahd something to do with money, but it's actually people as payroll involves people that are being paid to work. There wasn't any guidance on how to interpret the data so I also thought that affected intuitiveness.

Aesthetics: 8/10

I gave this visualization a fairly high score because it's a very straightforward color palette and layout, and I didn't have any major issues in terms of the aesthetics of it affecting my interpretation of it. I also thought it was very on brand for Bloomberg.

Engagement: 7/10

I found it to be a fairly strong conversation starter in terms of its conversation about the job market, which is heavily affecting people right now and the possibilities that might come out of understanding how payroll trends are different from estimates.

Wireframing

The first thing that I immediately knew I wanted to do in terms of wireframing was figuring out how to represent it in a line graph instead of a bar chart. I thought a line graph might be better because it would be easier to show trends and observe the difference between the two measures. I also wanted to change the y-axis labeling to be on the left side so it would make more sense with the placement of the legend.

Testing the Solution

I talked to two different people in order to test my solution and showed them all three wireframes to get feedback on what they liked and didn't like. The first person I talked to was a college student in her 20s. I was able to gain a few key insights from her interview.

For things she liked:

  • She thought that the line graph was effective in helping her understand that she was supposed to interpret the trend over time.

  • She also thought the color palette being simple made the visual not distracting.

  • Of the three wireframes, she liked the format of the legend for Wireframe 3 the best, and also liked the more teal color scheme.

For things she didn't like:

  • She didn't like the extreme blue of Wireframes 1 and 2 because she thought it was a bit too bright.

  • She remarked she was unfamiliar with what payroll meant and didn't completely understand what the numbers were representing.

The second person I talked to was an engineer in his 30s.

For things he liked:

  • He liked the horizontal lines better than the vertical lines for being able to keep track of the payroll data.

  • He also liked the line graph for seeing the trends in the data.

For things he didn't like:

  • He was also confused by the payroll data and what exactly it meant in terms of dollars versus jobs.

Build Your Solution

I ultimately decided on a solution that included teal coloring, clearer labels for the title, legend, and y-axis, and the same shading in order to emphasize the difference between the actual values and the estimate. I also ended up keeping the horizontal lines instead of the vertical lines because that made things clearer in terms of keeping up with the trends of the payroll numbers.
